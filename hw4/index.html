Part 1: Masses and Springs

In this part, we set up the grid of point masses connected by springs to represent the cloth. Each mass is places evenly across the defined width and height of the cloth. We configure the positions depending on if the cloth is oriented horizontally or vertically. We also mark certain masses as pinned depending on the users inputed specifications. After populating the grid with masses, we connected them with springs that enforce structural, shearing, and/or bending contrainsts. This ensures the cloth behaves realistically during simulations.

Part 2: Simulation Via Numerical Integration

In this part, we built a cloth simulation by integrating physical equations of motion to the the mass and spring model from part 1. In our representation, there are two kinds of forces: external forces (such as gravity) which uniformly affect the cloth and spring correction forces which apply the spring constraints from before to keep the cloth together.  We used the Verlet integration to integrate equations of motion. This allowed us to update positons of the point masses over time while applying the two kinds of forces. Additionally, to control the springs from becoming unrealisticaly deformed at each time step, we implement deformation contraints in the mass-spring model from the SIGGRAPH 1995 Provot paper .

Spring Constant (ks): With a low spring constant (ks), the springs are softer/less stiff. This behaves as such since the restorative force (computed using Hooke’s Law) is weak even if a spring is being stretched or compressed a lot from its resting length. The cloth moves very loosely, as if it is a soft fabric that is not very structurally strong, causing it to show more folds. From start to rest the cloth falls quickly and bounces more when it reaches the rest position. The cloth sags much more under gravity and lower since theres not enough strength/tension to hold the cloth at the pin points. We can observe the opposite happening with a higher ks, in which the fabric appears stiffer, does not sag as heavily (thus less folds), bounces less upon reaching its lowest point, moves slower from start to rest. This is all attributed to the idea that the restorative force is strong even for small movements/displacements.

Density: Changing the density of the cloth affects the mass of each point. With a low density, the point masses are lighter so we see less of an affect by gravity, and thus it sags less. With lighter masses, the cloth is then also sensitive to external forces and responds quickly, so the cloth appears light and airy. This also causes the cloth to ripple more easily. From start to rest position we can see that the cloth doesn’t hang as low since there’s less weight pulling down the cloth from the pin points. Additionally upon the initial fall of the cloth, we can see the cloth maintain its flat structure. We observe the opposite occur with a high density; we see the cloth sag more at the final resting position, the cloth looses its shape quickly upon falling (especially towards the center of the pin points), sags more dramatically, the cloth drapes and folds as heavy fabrics do.

Damping: Damping controls the loss of energy of an oscillating system by dissipation. Low damping means the energy remains in the system longer, so cloth takes much longer to reach its final resting position as it continues to oscillate. Since theres still much energy once the cloth reaches the bottom, the cloth bounces, bends, and twists; there are ripples that appear due to these movements. The cloth appears elastic with low damping. With high damping the cloth looses its energy faster, so once the cloth is closer to its final position, it moves slowly, indicating it reaches its steady state faster and with minimal oscillation. There are virtually no ripples at high damping values since theres no excessive movement that occurs as with low damping. The cloth almost appears stiff.

Part 3: Handling Collisions with Other Objects

Handling Collisions with Spheres: We first will add support for cloth collision with spheres. At a high level, we will determine whether or not a given point mass of the cloth is inside the sphere. If it is, we will adjust the point masses position such that it lies just outside the sphere, while accounting for friction. My implementation of sphere collision, takes in a single point mass of the cloth and updates its position if necessary. We first test if the point mass is inside the sphere by testing if the length of sphere’s radius is greater than the distance from the point mass to the sphere’s center. If the point masses position is inside the sphere, then we compute where the point mass should have intersected the sphere by extending the path between the point’s position and the sphere’s center to the sphere’s surface. To find this “tangent’ position, we can use this equation: center + normalized(direction vector)∗radius\text{center + normalized(direction vector)} * \text{radius}center + normalized(direction vector)∗radius﻿. Instead of directly snapping the current position of the point mass to this updated tangent position, we compute a correction vector to be applied to the point mass’s last_position in order to reach the tangent point. We do this by simply subtracting the last position from the tangent position. This correction is scaled dow by the friction factor using (1−f)(1-f)(1−f)﻿ so that not all energy is retained. We can finally update the mass’s position to be its last position plus this scaled correction.

We tested final resting positions of a cloth-sphere collision with different cloth spring constants (ks = 5000, 500, 50000). We can observe that the elasticity/flexibility of the cloth decreases as we increase ks. With a low spring constant, the springs become “soft” and the cloth drapes more dramatically over the sphere. The cloth hangs loose, saggy, and wrinkly over the sphere. The force of gravity is dominating over the restorative forces of the springs, causing the cloth to stretch and deform. The opposite occurs with a high ks value. We notice that the cloth does not hang loosly, and instead maintains its size and shape much better. We also notice that the cloth does not cover the sphere tightly as it does with a low ks. The restorative forces of the cloth are able to hold up against the force of gravity, making it noticeable that the cloth would be a more rigid material. We also notice that the cloth has less folds that are much larger due to the stiff springs. A moderate ks value, finds a balence between the two. However, all seem realistic to me since there is a wide varity of cloths that can exhibit each of these behaviors.

Handling Collisions with Planes: We will use the same logic as sphere collision for the cloth-plane collision implementation. We will consider a point mass “inside” the plane if the point moves from one side of the plane to the other in the last time step. If this occurs then we, similarly, need to “bump” the point mass back to the original side of the plane the cloth came from. We first need to test if a point crosses the plane. We compute signed distances of both the point mass’s last and current positions relative to a known point on the plane by using the plane’s normal. If one of these dot products is negative and the other positive, or if one is 0 then we say the point mass crossed the plane. We now compute the intersection point using the distance along the plane’s normal. The computed value ddd﻿ is then used to shift the current position of the point mass along the normal to obtain the “tangent” position: d∗normal vector+point mass positiond * \text{normal vector} + \text{point mass position} d∗normal vector+point mass position﻿. We then compute the correction vector, by this time offset upwards by a small displacement constant along the normal. Finally, we can update the mass’s position to be its last position updated by the correction vector and scaled down by friction.

Part 4: Handling Self Collisions

To address the cloth’s self collision, we will implement spatial hashing to efficiently detect and handle collisions between a cloth’s point masses, instead of testing each point mass with all other point masses for a collision in O(n2)O(n^2)O(n2)﻿.  At each time step, we will build a hash table (stored in the Cloth struct) to map a float to a vector of pointers to point masses. The floats uniquely represent a 3D box volume in the scene and its corresponding vector stores all the point masses that are contained in that 3D box volume. Once the map is built we just need to loop through all point masses, and for each point mass we look up the 3D box volume it is contained in (in constant time) and apply a repulsive collision force with the other point masses (if close enough in proximity) contained in its 3D box volume (stored in the corresponding vector).

We first created a function Cloth::hash_position that takes in a point mass’s position and returns a float that represents a specific 3D box volume. We do this by partitioning the 3D space into 3D boxes with dimensions w∗h∗tw*h*tw∗h∗t﻿ (www﻿ = 3 * plane width / num width points, hhh﻿ = 3 * plane height/ num height points, ttt﻿ = max(w, h)). The constant 3 is chosen to improve the accuracy of the spatial hashing algorithm. The hashing function I used is to the right. The returned value will be used as a unique key in our hash table.

double a = floor(pos.x/w);
double b = floor(pos.y/h);
double c = floor(pos.z/t); 
return (float)(a + 100*b + 100*100*c);

Next we build a spatial map with Cloth::build_spatial_map which loops over all point masses and use the Cloth::hash_position method to populate the map.
Next we implement
Cloth::self_collide which takes in a point mass and looks up potential candidates for collision using the hash table. For each candidate point mass we calculate the distance between it and the current point mass. If they are within 2∗thickness distance apart, we compute a correction vector that can be applied to the current point mass such that the two points would be 2∗thickness distance apart. The final correction vector for the current point mass will be an average of all the pairwise correction vectors (between it and all potential candidates for collision). We scale down this correction vector by simulation_steps to reduce the potential number of position corrections, thus helping improve accuracy.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader program is a collection of programs that run on the GPU and are written in a shading language (in our case GLSL, OpenGL Shading Language) . There are two mains kinds of shader programs: vertex shaders and fragment/pixel shaders. Vertex shaders handle positioning and transformation of vertices in a 3D scene. Fragment shaders determine color and properties of each pixel on a screen and are executed for each pixel rendered on a screen. These two programs work together, in which vertex shaders are first ran to transform positions and attributes from model space to clip space and also preps vertex data (normal vectors, tangents, texture coords) for interpolation. Vertex shaders then write the final positions of the vertex to gl_position and other variables to be used by the fragment shader. After rasterization, we end up with fragments are are processed by the fragment shaders. The fragment shaders take the geometric values calculated by the vertex shadrs and perform per pixel operations on them. This includes lighting, texture, and other material calculation/effects. The fragment shader will calculate an ouputted color using these material calculations.

The Blinn-Phong model approximates how light interacts with surfaces by combining three components (ambient, diffuse, and specular lighting). The ambient lighting is a constant term that simulates indirect light/global illumination in a scene. The diffuse lighting term depends on the angle between the surface normal and the light direction and simulates how light scatters on a rough surface, resulting in uniformly scattered light that is matte. Specular lighting produces shininess of a surface by modeling how light reflects a surface. It uses the half vector between the light and the viewing direction to the power of some shininess value.
